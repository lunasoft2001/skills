# Speaker Notes Format Guide

Style rules and timing benchmarks for `presentation-speaker-notes`.

---

## Speaking Pace Benchmarks

| Style | Words per minute | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `conversational` | 120 wpm | Natural dialogue pace; most business presentations |
| `formal` | 100 wpm | Measured, deliberate; regulatory, government, academic |
| `storytelling` | 110 wpm | Rhythm varies; includes pauses for effect |

**Timing formula:**
> Script words = duration_min × wpm

Example: 2-minute slide at conversational pace → 240 words

**Warn with ⏱️ when:**
- Slide script word count > `duration_min × wpm × 1.1` (10% over)
- Total script > presentation duration + 10%

---

## Per-Slide Script Principles

1. **Do not read the slide** — complement it. The script adds context, anecdotes, or emphasis that the slide cannot show.
2. **Start with the message** — the first sentence of each script should reinforce the slide's headline.
3. **Use concrete language** — prefer specific examples over abstract claims.
   - ❌ "We saw improvement in key areas."
   - ✅ "Churn dropped from 8% to 4.5% — that's 420 fewer customers lost per quarter."
4. **Signal transitions** — the last sentence of each script bridges to the next slide.
5. **Vary sentence length** — short sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences carry explanation.

## Style Guides

### Conversational
- Use contractions: "we've", "it's", "I'll"
- First-person plural: "we built", "our team"
- OK to start with "So," or "Now,"
- Direct address: "you'll notice on this chart..."

### Formal
- No contractions: "we have", "it is", "I will"
- Third-person or passive where appropriate: "the data indicates"
- Avoid colloquialisms
- Transitions: "I will now turn to...", "As demonstrated in the preceding slide..."

### Storytelling
- Open with a scene or anecdote before the data
- Use the present tense for immediacy: "It's March 2025. We have 60 days left..."
- Build tension before the resolution
- End the story before the Q&A begins

## Q&A Preparation Rules

**Question sourcing:**
- Derive questions from the storyboard's content, particularly: numbers, decisions, risks, comparisons, and anything described as "new" or "first."
- Flag budget, headcount, and risk questions with ⚠️ for diplomatic framing.

**Diplomatic framing templates:**
- "That's an important nuance — let me make sure I give you an accurate answer after the session."
- "We're still finalizing that — I can share a preliminary view: ..."
- "I don't have that figure with me today, but I can follow up by end of week."

**Closing statement structure:**
1. Thank the audience for their time and engagement.
2. Restate the single most important action or takeaway.
3. Provide contact info or next-step logistics.

Example closing:
> "Thank you — this has been a great conversation. The one thing I want you to leave with today is [key message]. We'll follow up with the summary deck by Thursday. Please reach out directly if you have questions in the meantime."

## Pre-Presentation Checklist (always include)

```markdown
## Pre-presentation checklist
- [ ] Test slides on the room's display (resolution, fonts, colors)
- [ ] Confirm clicker / remote is paired and working
- [ ] Have a glass of water at the podium or within reach
- [ ] Know the first sentence by heart — start confident without looking at notes
- [ ] Confirm the room layout (seated? standing? hybrid audience?)
- [ ] Know the moderator's or host's name
- [ ] Have the Q&A cutoff time agreed with the moderator
```
